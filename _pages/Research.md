My research combines globally distributed observations from the Surface Particulate Matter Network (SPARTAN) with chemical transport modeling (GEOS-Chem) to improve the understanding of aerosol composition, evaluate emission inventories, and develop measurement techniques for global air quality and health applications.

**Offline Aerosol Mass Spectrometry Methodology for Organic Aerosol Characterization** (<a href="https://doi.org/10.5194/egusphere-2026-2663" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ren et al., <i>Atmos. Meas. Tech.</i>, 2026</a>)

Organic aerosol (OA) measurements remain sparse globally because online aerosol mass spectrometers are expensive and difficult to deploy at long-term monitoring sites. We developed a reproducible offline Aerosol Mass Spectrometry (AMS) method for quantifying OA composition from particulate matter collected on Teflon filters. The method requires only small extract volumes, achieves high nebulization efficiency and low detection limits, and is directly compatible with SPARTAN. By extending AMS measurements to archived filter samples and monitoring networks worldwide, this methodology enables construction of a long-term, globally consistent OA dataset for evaluating atmospheric models and understanding aerosol sources.

**Black Carbon Emissions Generally Underestimated in the Global South** (<a href="https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-62468-5" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ren et al., <i>Nat. Comm.</i>, 2025</a>)

Large uncertainties in black carbon (BC) emissions persist because observations remain sparse across much of the world. Using globally distributed SPARTAN measurements together with high-resolution GEOS-Chem simulations, we evaluated contemporary global BC emission inventories. We found that the Community Emissions Data System (CEDS) reproduces BC variability across many developed regions but substantially underestimates concentrations throughout much of the Global South. These results identify regions where emission inventories require improvement and demonstrate the importance of expanding long-term global aerosol observations.

**Bioaccessibility and Health Risks of Heavy Metals in Particulate Matter** (<a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scitotenv.2024.174303" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ren et al., <i>Sci. Total Environ.</i>, 2024</a>; <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chemosphere.2021.130312" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ren et al., <i>Chemosphere</i>, 2021</a>)

Health risks associated with particulate matter depend not only on the abundance of toxic metals but also on their bioaccessibility after inhalation. We investigated the concentrations, bioaccessibility, and emission sources of PM-bound heavy metal(loid)s across twelve Chinese cities and evaluated their associated health risks. Our results revealed substantial variability among individual metals and emission sources, highlighting the importance of incorporating bioaccessibility into exposure assessment and supporting more effective, source-oriented air pollution control strategies.
